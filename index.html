A Brief History of Cartesian Dualism

René Descartes

Born in 1596, René Descartes was a 17th-century French philosopher. He was broadly recognized for his contributions to mathematics and science, with well-known works and discoveries including:

  • The Cartesian Plane
  • The Scientific Method/Mechanistic Philosophy
  • Preliminary work on Newton's Laws of Motion: The Three Laws Of Nature

Mind-Body Dualism

Descartes was particularly well known for his theory of mind and body: mind-body dualism. This was a metaphysical theory about how the mind and body should be thought about logically, asserting that the mind and body were two distinct entitites which work together, with the body being material in nature and the mind being an immaterial substance. The mind was of course capable of thought, and was not physically extended (able to take up space), while the body is. He defends this mind-body distinction further, stating that the body is divisible into parts, while the mind is not.(citation here) This will become important later.

Strengths of Dualism

While they get a lot of flak these days, Descartes' ideas about the relationship between body and mind were very impactful in their time. Dualism was a departure from the dominant and restrictive Orthodox Christian ideologies of the time, which viewed the mind and body as a unified extension of the soul. With this, dissection of the human body was very rare, since it was thought that bodies needed to be preserved in order to go to heaven. Additionally, it was believed that disease and illness was a result of moral wrongdoings, as a sort of punishment (Mehta 203).

By separating the nonmaterial(mind and/or soul) from the material, Descartes' new theory laid the groudwork for modern biomedical thought. Dissection of bodies became much more common, leading to huge advancements in fields like anatomy and physiology (Mehta 203). This logical new way of viewing the body mechanistically, as biological system of parts, equipped scientists and doctors with the tools to understand and treat basic diseases and biological dysfunctions. In many ways, dualism was the introduction of the scientific method to Western medicine.
